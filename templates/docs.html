XPDRC 1.0 Algorithm Documentation

⬅ Back to UI

XPDRC (Cross-Platform Digital Room Correction) is an advanced DSP generation tool that communicates directly with the Room EQ Wizard (REW) API to extract raw impulse responses (IR). By processing the native impulse responses generated by REW without intermediate text or waveform conversions, XPDRC generates zero-latency minimum-phase or linear-phase Convolution FIR filters.

This documentation outlines the exact mechanics of the processing algorithms executed by XPDRC, separated into default behaviors and optional/advanced methodologies.

1. Default Active Processing

The following steps represent the default correction pipeline applied to the acoustic measurements. These defaults are explicitly optimized to provide the highest-fidelity correction and phase adherence while meticulously avoiding artifact generation (such as pre-ringing, inversion ringing, or unnatural room-field removal).

Frequency-Dependent Windowing (FDW)

Correcting a loudspeaker in a room poses a fundamental problem: a microphone captures both the direct anechoic response of the speaker and the diffuse, lingering reverb of the room. Attempting to indiscriminately invert this combined signal over-corrects the mid-to-high frequencies, leading to an audibly sterile and unnatural sound.

XPDRC employs Frequency-Dependent Windowing (FDW), defaulting to 5 cycles. At any given frequency f, the raw impulse response is isolated by evaluating a temporal window of 5 / f seconds centered directly on the impulse peak.

  • At 10,000 Hz, the window is only 0.5 ms, capturing exclusively the initial speaker transient (direct field) before the first room reflection can even hit the microphone.
  • At 100 Hz, the window is 50 ms. This acknowledges that bass frequencies inherently couple with the room boundary, effectively making the room part of the sound source.
  • Why default to 5 cycles? Aggressive windowing (e.g., 2 cycles) removes too much midrange energy and distorts magnitude targeting, while overly wide windowing (e.g., 15 cycles) encompasses too many early reflections. 5 cycles represents the psychoacoustic "sweet spot" to smooth combat-comb-filtering without stripping crucial tonal body.

Hard-Clipped Magnitude Inversion & Sub-Schroeder Priority

By default, XPDRC calculates the required EQ by taking the inverse response `T / H_mag` (where T is the target curve level, and H_mag is the smoothed measurement amplitude).

To prevent infinite-gain singularity when speakers suffer acoustic nulls (dips), XPDRC employs strict bounds limiting (e.g., maximum +6dB low, +3dB high). Acoustic nulls resulting from quarter-wave boundary phase-cancellations cannot be filled with power. Pumping energy into them only wastes amplifier headroom and significantly increases distortion. Limited clipping mitigates these anomalies.

Subwoofers (Sub-Schroeder Frequencies): Below the room's Schroeder frequency (the transition between standing wave modal zones and ray-acoustics reflection zones), FDW isn't appropriate. XPDRC defaults to evaluating the full long impulse response in the modal region, identifying broad acoustic peaks and modal ringing to apply deep, high-Q cuts without overboost penalty.

Excess Phase Linearization

A standard loudspeaker crossover array is, by definition, a minimum-phase system, meaning changes in frequency magnitude correlate directly with lag in temporal phase deployment (group delay). Higher frequencies fire first, followed mathematically by delayed lower frequencies.

XPDRC linearizes the system by calculating the Excess Phase. Using complex cepstrum liftering, XPDRC derives the purely Minimum-Phase theoretical response of the measured IR magnitude. By dividing the actual measured complex response by this minimum-phase component, the unwrap of the remainer provides the exact `Excess Phase`. XPDRC creates an inverse filter (H_mag * exp(-1j * excess_phase)) that artificially rewinds the phase rotation back to perfectly causal 0-degrees alignment, ensuring transients (like a snare drum) hit the DAC output completely time-aligned across the entire spectrum.

Iterative Subwoofer Group Delay (GD) EQ

Subwoofers, due to physical box tuning (such as bass-reflex ports), feature tremendous Group Delay. The time lag is audible as bas overhang or "smearing". Standard EQ magnitude inversions do not fix this lag temporally.

XPDRC's default subwoofer algorithm applies specific negative excess-phase timing adjustments to pull bass frequencies temporally forward. However, brute-forcing absolute flat group delay in a room causes algorithmic instability; the inverted filter will create sharp, violent "ringing" artifacts oscillating prior to the bass transient.

XPDRC utilizes Iterative Ringing Analysis. It generates a trial filter, parses the resulting convolution for pre-ringing or post-ringing amplitude crests beyond a safety threshold, and dials back the phase-correction smoothing and aggressiveness recursively until it converges on a mathematically safe filter that tightens bass significantly without introducing inaudible distortion signatures.

Automatic Time and Volume Alignment

By extracting the literal `timeOfIRPeakSeconds` from the REW APIs, XPDRC calculates precisely how physically distant your speakers are from the listening position. It identifies the furthest acoustic anchor and delays all adjacent speakers automatically at the sample-level.

Additionally, it parses the 1/3-octave smoothed amplitude between 500Hz-2000Hz (the region where human hearing is most linear) to establish RMS equivalents, and applies precise inverse decibel scaling across all output FIR vectors so every channel operates at precisely the same acoustic volume at the listening position.

Pre-Ringing Compensation (PRC) (Default: On unless Mixed-Phase enabled)

When computing standard Excess-Phase linearization, any inverted high-Q null corrections result in infinite-impulse ringing backwards in time. The PRC algorithm scans the newly calculated filter backwards from the peak.

Below the configured transition parameter (default: 1000Hz), PRC applies aggressive 1/3-octave phase smoothing, destroying narrow high-Q phase corrections but maintaining the macro phase rotation. The result is a Linear-Phase impulse where macro-timings are snapped to 0 degrees, but temporal ringing artifacts generated by individual room reflections are ironed out and discarded before generation.

Virtual Bass Array (VBA)

A VBA utilizes destructive acoustical interference rather than electrical EQ adjustment. By detecting exact prominent frequency nodes (standing waves), the DSP generates an inverted secondary electrical pulse delayed by the exact boundary transit time of the room. When the front subwoofer's bass energy reflects off the back wall and returns to the listener (creating a boom), the secondary delayed pulse fires, arriving exactly at the same time completely out-of-phase, annihilating and absorbing the reflective boundary energy in real-time.

2. Advanced & Toggleable Algorithms

The following are powerful but optional configurations within XPDRC that fundamentally change the filter design philosophy. They are disabled by default because they require careful interpretation of listening parameters.

Mixed-Phase Design (Default: Off)

Full-range Linearization causes "pre-ringing", a reverse-echo where transient build-up occur before the source sound in absolute time. If applied to deep bass nulls, this pre-ringing stretches into audibility.

Mixed-Phase decomposing explicitly forces the filter to remain completely Minimum-Phase (causal, no pre-ringing, fully mathematically native to speaker properties) below a certain designated threshold (default: 500Hz). Above this threshold, it employs a soft one-octave Hann window crossfade into Linear-Phase (transient perfect response). This offers the "best of both worlds," maximizing treble staging while leaving bass tight and free of temporal reverse-smearing. When enabled, it supersedes PRC.

Kirkeby Regularized Spectral Inversion (Default: Off)

Instead of hard bounds-clipping the inverse magnitude, Kirkeby utilizes Tikhonov regularization applied iteratively across the spectrum in the format: eq = (Target * H) / (H^2 + beta_f^2).

XPDRC utilizes an advanced psychoacoustic shaping vector for `beta_f`. Rather than a flat variable, `beta_f` is intensely aggressive in the least-audible ranges (sub-20Hz, overriding extreme boosts) and gentle in the sensitive human speech band (200Hz - 5kHz). It creates softer, broader sweeping EQ interactions instead of hard mathematical cuts. However, because it actively allows null-filling under smooth mathematical rules, it requires extensive headroom scaling, which reduces overall global output volume heavily. Thus, it remains an opt-in toggle.

Recommendations for Best Practices

A Note on Defaults: XPDRC’s default settings are precisely engineered to yield the highest objective sound quality with minimal artifacts. We strongly urge new users to fully utilize the default processing pipeline first (FDW, Excess Phase Linearization, PRC, and Automatic Alignment). Toggleable options like Kirkeby regularization or Mixed-Phase design dramatically alter generation math and should only be applied after carefully establishing and evaluating a default baseline correction in your room.
